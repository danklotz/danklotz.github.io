Quotes: Sunset Salvo

In the following are some memorable quotes from John W. Tukey’s speech sunset salvo.

Regarding the process of data analysis:

My title is borrowed from Estil green who used it when he retired as Executive Vice-President at Bell Laboratories about two decades ago. He was an executive, so he really retired.

The simplicty of wrting thesis in, and assessing the correctess of, mathematical statistics […] will aways ensure expansion of that corner. So our chief struggle has to be to emphasize all the other directions.

… but remember no fixed data-driven process can allow for systematic errors!

On graphics:

Figure 1 is an old-fashioned plot, designed to go with the pitch, “O, see how straight!” Good for propagande, poor for careful study.

“A picture can be worth 1,00 words, but to be so, it may have to include 100 words.”

Talking about theory proving:

The usefulness of the latter usually suffers, perhaps necessarily, from constrained, unrealistic hypotheses. […]. As a consequence, theorem provid is seductive - and its Lorelei voices can be put us on the rocks.

In a world in which theprice of calculation continues to decrease rapidly, but the price of theorem proving continues to hold stead or increase, elemnatry economics indicates that we ought to spend a larger and larger fraciton of our time on calculation.

On asymptotic theory:

A hard look at the practice and results of conventional asymptotic theory easily leads one to three points: 1. We ought not to expect it to be widely useful (since it refers to the ultimate oversimplification). 2. Rather often it has been useful (surprise!). 3. WE hav edeveloped no basis for telling whenit is likely to help and when it is not; …

On the culture of data analysis:

Hubris is the greatest danger that accompanies formal data analysis, including formalized statistical analysis.

The data may not contain the answer. The combination of some data and an aching desire for an answer does not ensure that a reasonable answer can be extracted from a body of data.

As the gold becomes harder to find, better experiments - particularly more randomization and more runs per experiment- will be needed and reeducation wil be required, but it may well still be cheapter and quicker to do the early stages on an exploratory basis, wheter admittedly so or not.

It is dangerous hubris to think that we can solve all of the world’s problems; but we can help many people in many fields, altough what we can do is limited.
