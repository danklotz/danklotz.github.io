SC - Vogel and Reichenstein

Short comments on two papers:

Vogel (2017): Stochastic watershed models for hydrologic risk management

This paper provides a - perhaps too enthusiastic - overview of the potential uses of stochastic watershed models (for hydrological risk management). It goes trough some of history and explains why they are seemingly underused today. It’s a good read, despite being a bit lengthy. It seemed to be that the information could be condensed more and poured into a well rounded opinion peace. From what I can tell the references are well chosen, albeit some of the made claims miss them.

Reichstein et al. (2019): Deep learning and process understanding for data-driven Earth system science

This is some sort of position-paper about the (potential) use of modern ML for environmental applications. The scholarship seems to be sloppy; at least from a hydrological perspective. I was often not able to deduce why a particular reference is used at a given point. And, I honestly beieve that this is the fault of the authors and not of my faulty comprehension skills. Additionally - as mentioned by @fkratzert - the paper seems to be a bit outdated (already). Most of the references are from before 201z, missing some crucial publications that appeared since then. I take that as an indication that the earth sciences adapt the methods faster than the authors believe (despite the many inherent difficulties of applying ML methods to the field).

That said, I like the big-picture painted by the article. The authors put a large focus on infusing domain knowledge into the algorithms, e.g. in the form of hybrid-models (this is, for example, historical references to environmental hybrid models are missing). They also mention the importance of uncertainty quantification and the difficulty of data quality and quantities. All things that lie near to my heart.
